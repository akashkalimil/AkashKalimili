Translaid

Gesture to Speech Translation

Aim of the project was to create an effective communication method that breaks down language barrier.

What does it do?

Register Hand gestures using a Myo armband which is fetched by an Arduino UNO board and translated into text to be read by a processing script that displays speech. Not only does our program output to our laptop's display and its sound system, but we also outputted to an external lcd to show the possibilities of implementing a mobile product with its sole processor.

Challenges we faced

Getting familiar with Myo Armband API and usage. Calibration of the Myo Armband. Producing input that could be fetched by the Arduino Uno board. Google Translated was attempted however Google Translate API was not free to public consumers. Mac El Capitan OS X had intriguing serial encoding that was going on, so that it was hard to recognize data sent over the internal serial channels.

Accomplishments that we're proud of

We accomplished our set goals. We were able to work from different start point and join the project together in an agile environment.

What we learned

LUA scripting, Myo Armband API, Processing, Arduino, Teamwork, Organization, Shared passion for Digiflare.

Wireless Inertial Navigation System

Research Project

Using an accelerometer as a source of data, I designed and built analog signal conditioning circuitry to optimize the signal for digitization and serialization. After building ADC circuitry and Serialization Circuitry, I ran many diagnostic tests for baud rate and data loss with oscilloscopes to make sure I wasn't over sampling or undersampling. I used an RN-41 Bluetooth module. I used a microcontroller (Ardiuno) to program program protocols on the flash memory of the chip. On the recieving end of the connection, an FPGA (Nexys 5) was hooked up to the reciever. Using Xilinx , I implemented integration algorithms that I created. My solution for integration drift was numerous trials to calculate constants. At the end of the year, I presented this project at the annual TJStar Research Symposium (Large event where research projects are presented to the school community. Another image is of a portable demonstration, I constructed, with a Nexus 5 as a display for the TJSTAR Presentation.

Advanced Analog, Digital, Audio, and Microprocessor Electronics

I worked with 6800 series Motorola Microprocessor and learned microprocessor architecture. I also designed and programmed the processor in assembly for a specific function. Other components of this project include ROMs, Counters, Logic Gates. During Advanced Audio Electronics course, used previous microprocessor circuit for DSP. Later on, I also worked with a gate array for DSP. The image below is a schematic, I designed, of the original processor circuit w/o any additional components which I have later created to act as supplements for the original design.

In Advanced Analog Electronics, I worked with operational amplifiers, transistors, and RC filters to design a decent quality audio amplifier. I then worked with PSpice and Circuit Lab to design a circuit that modulates multiple signals with one signal path and respectively demodulates them. Using configurations such as diode detectors, I developed AM modulation and demodulation circuits.

In Advanced Digital Electronics, I worked with inverters, and gates, or gates, nor gates to design an application with a multiplexed led display. I designed with up/down counters, decoders, and jfet transistors. I also used ROMs for my application and I had a rom for music storage. I developed with DACs for a source of audio.

In Advanced Audio Electronics, I worked with ADCs and DACs for DSP of an analog signal. At first I was working with discrete circuitry, but then I started working with FPGAs and CPLDs for the DSP component of the circuit. I also studied discrete fourier transform during the course.
